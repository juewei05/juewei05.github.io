Seeing Around Corners Without a Corner

Via Natural Scene Encoding, we no longer need a configured occluder to achieve passive NLOS imaging.

June 16, 2026   ·  8 min read

This work was conducted as my undergraduate thesis at USTC, supervised by Prof. Feihu Xu. This post was drafted with AI assistance and revised by the author.

1. Introduction #

Every imaging system has a line-of-sight limit. Breaking it is the goal of non-line-of-sight (NLOS) imaging. The applications span autonomous driving, search-and-rescue, military reconnaissance, and medical endoscopy.

Many NLOS work takes the active route: fire an ultrafast laser, measure photon time-of-flight, reconstruct the hidden scene. Impressive results, but the hardware is expensive, and shining a laser announces your presence.

Passive NLOS uses only ambient light, no active illumination. Cheaper, stealthier, and much harder: without time-of-flight, you are left with nearly only intensity information.

Nearly all existing passive methods share a hidden assumption: they need a man-made occluder, like a wall corner or doorframe, to cast distinguishable shadows. This limits where the technique can work. What if a scene has no convenient corner?

This was the question behind my undergraduate thesis: do we actually need a separate occluder? Everyday environments are full of structure, from book stacks to curtain folds to crumpled paper. Could these natural objects themselves serve as the encoder?

The answer is yes. The longer story, involving three experiments and a diagnostic that upended our initial expectations, is what this post is about.

2. The Core Idea #

Physical Intuition #

Imagine a hidden LED behind an obstacle. Its light scatters off surfaces in the visible scene and reaches a camera. Move the LED, and the shadows shift. If the visible scene is a flat wall, the shift is subtle: poor encoding. If it is geometrically rich, with boxes, plants, or crumpled paper, each LED position casts a dramatically different shadow pattern: good encoding.

The central insight: everyday scene geometry does exactly what a man-made occluder does, selectively blocking and scattering light from different directions.

The Linear Model #

Discretize the hidden scene into $N$ point sources and the camera into $M$ pixels. The relationship is linear:

$$ \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} $$

$\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{N}$: hidden source intensities (what we reconstruct). $\mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{R}^{M}$: camera measurements. $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times N}$: the light transport matrix (LTM). Column $i$ of $\mathbf{A}$ is the camera image when only the $i$-th hidden source is on.

Condition Number #

Encoding quality is captured by the condition number $\kappa$:

$$ \kappa(\mathbf{A}) = \frac{\sigma_{\max}}{\sigma_{\min}} $$

Low $\kappa$ means columns are well-separated: different source positions produce easily-distinguishable patterns. High $\kappa$ means columns look alike, making reconstruction sensitive to noise. Scene geometry drives $\kappa$: complex structures produce diverse responses (low $\kappa$); flat surfaces produce redundant ones (high $\kappa$).

3. Calibration: The Hadamard Encoding Trick #

To reconstruct, we need $\mathbf{A}$. 1 The intuitive approach, turning on one source at a time, is SNR-limited: a single LED is too dim in passive conditions.

We use Hadamard encoding. Instead of one LED at a time, we illuminate with $\pm 1$ patterns where roughly half the LEDs are on simultaneously, take a complementary pair (swap on/off), and subtract to cancel ambient light and dark current. After $N$ pattern pairs, we recover:

$$ \hat{\mathbf{A}} = \frac{1}{N} \Delta\mathbf{Y} \mathbf{H}^\top $$

Relative to sequential scanning, this gives an SNR gain of $\sqrt{N}/2$, which is $8\times$ for our $N=256$ source positions. A critical detail: the differential calibration requires background-subtracted validation measurements. Without this consistency, ambient bias gets amplified by $\kappa$ and destroys reconstruction.

We used two solvers. Conjugate Gradient Descent (CGD) provides pure numerical inversion without regularization, ideal for observing the $\kappa$-quality relationship. SPIRALTAP with total variation (TV) regularization adds a spatial smoothness prior that helps on noisy real data.

4. Simulation: The Ideal World #

We first tested the idea in Blender Cycles. Four scenes of increasing geometric complexity: sphere, Stanford bunny, plant, and Gundam model. Everything else held constant (same diffuse material, camera, LED layout). 2

The results were clean:

Sphere62.412.72

$\kappa$ and PSNR showed a strict negative monotonic relationship. Encoding pattern choice was secondary: scene geometry dominated. These simulations demonstrate that natural scene encoding works in an ideal linear world.

5. Real Experiments: When Theory Meets Practice #

We built a physical setup: a 16×16 LED array (the hidden “scene” with known ground truth), a ZWO ASI585MC camera with a 50mm F1.4 lens, and four everyday objects as encoding surfaces: stacked cardboard boxes, a crumpled paper ball, a folded umbrella, and a fumo plush toy.

Here is where things got interesting:

Scene$\kappa$PSNR (dB)SSIM
Fumo18.711.630.475
Umbrella132.79.320.248
Paper149.211.740.390
Box212.314.360.550

The fumo has the best $\kappa$ (18.7) but middling reconstruction. The cardboard box has the worst $\kappa$ (212.3) but the best reconstruction. The clean monotonic relationship from simulation was gone.

What happened? Beyond $\kappa$, real scenes differ in signal strength (the umbrella’s modulation depth barely cleared the noise floor), material properties (the fumo’s plush surface introduces subsurface scattering that violates the Lambertian assumption), and model fidelity (Pearson $r$ between measured $\mathbf{y}$ and $\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}_{\text{gt}}$ varied from 0.972 to 0.998 across scenes). $\kappa$ quantifies encoding potential, but it cannot capture everything that matters in a real system.

6. Synthetic Experiments: The Diagnosis #

To isolate whether the problem was in the encoding matrices or in external physical factors, we ran a diagnostic. We kept the real $\mathbf{A}$ from each scene but generated synthetic measurements:

$$ \mathbf{y}{\text{syn}} = \mathbf{A},\mathbf{x}{\text{gt}} + \epsilon, \quad \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2) $$

This eliminates material nonlinearity, ambient light, and other real-world confounders while preserving the actual encoding matrix. If $\kappa$ and PSNR correlate here, the matrices are fine; if not, they are the problem.

Scene$\kappa$CGD PSNR (1% noise)CGD PSNR (5% noise)
Fumo18.741.1 dB27.5 dB
Umbrella132.728.5 dB20.3 dB
Paper149.228.0 dB18.5 dB
Box212.327.8 dB18.0 dB

The monotonic relationship reappeared. Fumo, with its excellent $\kappa$, crushed the other scenes (41.1 dB at 1% noise). Box, with its terrible $\kappa$, ranked last.

For these four scenes, the real $\mathbf{A}$ matrices encode well. The problem in real experiments is not the encoding matrices themselves but the physical factors (subsurface scattering, ambient light, weak signal) that the linear model does not describe.

7. Ambient Light: Not Just Additive Noise #

We also tested whether ambient room light could be removed by post-processing. With lights on, Pearson $r$ values dropped: box (0.994 → 0.959), paper (0.998 → 0.852), umbrella (0.998 → 0.692). We tried every debiasing method available (mean subtraction, Gaussian blur, SVD, GoDec). The best gain was +2.15 dB on the box scene, and the result remained visually unrecognizable.

Ambient light cannot be treated as simple additive noise. When it dominates, $\mathbf{A}$ no longer describes what the camera sees and the linear model loses validity around $r \approx 0.95$. Whether ambient light changes the transport process itself or simply introduces effects beyond the linear model remains an open question.

8. What We Learned #

The three experiments tell a coherent story. Simulation validated the core concept: natural scene geometry can encode hidden information, and $\kappa$ is a valid proxy for encoding quality in ideal conditions. Real experiments showed that $\kappa$ alone cannot predict cross-scene performance: signal strength, material properties, and model fidelity matter just as much. The synthetic experiment exonerated the encoding matrices and pointed at external physical factors as the real bottleneck.

This three-tier design, moving from ideal simulation through messy reality to a controlled diagnostic, is itself a useful methodological pattern for computational imaging: it separates “is the concept sound?” from “why doesn’t it work in practice?” without conflating the two.

Among our four test scenes, three factors appeared to matter: approximate Lambertian surfaces (check via Pearson $r$), adequate geometric structure for encoding (moderate $\kappa$), and sufficient signal return above the noise floor. The cardboard box struck the best balance despite having the worst $\kappa$.

Future directions include hardware upgrades (larger aperture, lower-noise detectors), adaptive Hadamard encoding that allocates more measurements to weak channels, and better priors beyond TV regularization. Cross-paradigm validation on classic corner/occluder configurations would provide a quantitative bridge to prior work.

Key takeaways:

  • Natural scene geometry can replace artificial occluders for passive NLOS imaging.
  • $\kappa$ quantifies encoding potential but does not determine reconstruction quality alone in real systems.
  • The bottleneck is not encoding complexity; it is model fidelity and signal strength.
  • Ambient light degrades reconstruction in ways that simple additive debiasing cannot fix. The linear model’s validity under ambient light deserves further study.
